## Scope Of The Work

This work evaluates next-action selection under defective user inputs. The benchmark asks whether an assistant should answer, ask a clarification, challenge a false or stale premise, or abstain when support is insufficient. It is an evaluation of action calibration, not a deployment policy that assistants should become more confrontational in every setting.

The paper's intended claim is narrow: under the completed Day-1 local checkpoint matrix and prompt protocol, open instruction and reasoning checkpoints remain far from saturated on this action-selection objective. The work does not claim to solve hallucination, factuality, safety, or assistant policy in general.

## Data Provenance

- Current examples are converted from benchmark-style or curated public-source material, not from private user logs.
- The active Day-1 evidence bundle covers answerable controls, false-premise prompts, stale-premise prompts, conflicting-evidence questions, ambiguous-intent cases, and insufficient-evidence cases.
- The current empirical claims are scoped to the active Day-1 bundle. Ambiguous-intent and hard-abstain rows are paper-facing, but claims about clarification and abstention behavior should remain slice-scoped until source diversity and model-analysis depth are broadened.
- Candidate generation and promotion are handled through `scripts/promote_validated_expansion_candidates.py`; generated rows are paper-facing only after validation and promotion into the expanded split.
- The stale-premise slice uses public, checkable facts and is framed as a premise-defect task: the user prompt assumes an outdated fact and the helpful first action is to correct that premise before answering.

## Potential Harms

- Over-answering can reinforce false or stale assumptions, especially when a user prompt presupposes an incorrect fact.
- Over-challenging or over-abstaining can make assistants obstructive for ordinary answerable questions.
- A benchmark score could be misused as a generic safety score if the task scope is not stated clearly.
- Ambiguous-intent and insufficient-evidence cases are present in the paper-facing split, but thinner than the answer/challenge evidence base, so claims about clarification and abstention behavior must stay scoped.
- A model can score better on utility by becoming too cautious unless answer-supported accuracy, over-answer rate, and per-slice behavior are reported together.

## Mitigations In The Paper

- Report action accuracy, per-slice metrics, over-answer rate, answer-supported accuracy, JSON parse rate, and utility together rather than relying on utility alone.
- State that utility encodes an asymmetric harm ordering, not a universal user-cost model.
- Keep false-premise, stale-premise, answerable-control, and conflicting-evidence examples visible so readers can inspect boundary cases.
- State that the action ontology is a modeling choice and that alternate taxonomies remain future work.
- Scope the DeepSeek reasoning result to the completed local checkpoint matrix and prompt protocol.
- Treat `decision_first` as an exploratory calibration lever, not as a complete method or deployment-ready controller.
